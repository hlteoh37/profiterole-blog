When Does a Roth Conversion Make Sense?

A Roth IRA conversion is a taxable event — you pay income tax now on money you transfer from a Traditional IRA to a Roth IRA. In return, all future growth is tax-free and you owe no tax on qualified withdrawals in retirement.

Convert if (typically):

  • Your current tax bracket is lower than your expected retirement bracket
  • You have 10+ years for the Roth to compound tax-free
  • You can pay the conversion tax from non-IRA funds
  • You expect tax rates to rise broadly in the future
  • You want to reduce future Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs)

Skip conversion if:

  • You're in a higher bracket now than you expect in retirement
  • You'd need to use IRA funds to pay the conversion tax
  • Retirement is less than 5–7 years away
  • The conversion would push you into AMT or trigger IRMAA surcharges

The Roth 5-Year Rule

Each conversion amount has its own 5-year holding period before penalty-free withdrawal. Converting at age 58+ generally avoids this issue since you'll be 63 before touching converted funds.
